# silhouette-balance-principles.md

> Reference taxonomy of body shapes and balance-focused styling principles, consolidated from image consultancy, fashion stylist, costume design, and academic anthropometric sources. Designed for use by a photo-based shape-identification app. Tone is balance-focused, pose-neutral, and body-positive throughout.

## Sourcing and methodology header

This document synthesizes four bodies of literature: the academic FFIT (Female Figure Identification Technique) framework (Simmons, Istook & Devarajan 2004; Lee, Istook, Nam & Park 2007; Sokolowski & Bettencourt 2020); the 12-shape system from Trinny Woodall and Susannah Constantine's *The Body Shape Bible* (2007); the 7-letter system from Imogen Lamport's *Inside Out Style* (AICI-credentialed); and the 5-shape consensus represented in The Concept Wardrobe, 40+Style, and similar stylist publications. Costume design and illustration principles are drawn from Bina Abling's *Fashion Sketchbook*, Mathis & Connor's *The Triumph of Individual Style*, and Tortora & Eubank's *Survey of Historic Costume*.

**Conventions used in this document:**
- **Five canonical parents**: Hourglass, Pear, Inverted Triangle, Rectangle, Apple.
- **Variants** are nested under their canonical parent and described as deltas.
- **Numerical thresholds** use the FFIT inch-based system and the percentage-based "5% / 25%" stylist system in parallel.
- **"Use carefully"** replaces "avoid"; principles are framed as conditional, not prohibitive.
- **Diagnostic cues** are written for photo-based identification and prioritize observable proportions over self-report.

## 1. Hourglass

**ID:** `hourglass`
**Family:** primary
**Aliases:** X-shape, classic hourglass, balanced curvy

**How to identify:** Shoulders/bust and hips read as roughly equal width, with a clear inward indentation at the natural waist. The torso silhouette traces a symmetrical "X" from shoulder to thigh.
**Goal:** Maintain the existing balance and let the natural waist read clearly.

### Diagnostic cues
- Shoulder pixel-width and hip pixel-width within 5% of each other.
- Waist at least 25% narrower than bust and hips, or roughly 9 in / 23 cm smaller than bust and 10 in / 25 cm smaller than hips (FFIT thresholds).
- Waist-to-hip ratio approximately 0.70 to 0.75 (BodySpec benchmark).
- The narrowest visual point on the torso is the natural waist, midway between the lowest rib and the iliac crest.
- Bust prominence varies; bust size alone does not define this shape, and busty Rectangles are commonly misclassified as Hourglass when the waist is not actually indented to threshold.
- Limbs typically read in proportion with torso, neither notably slim nor notably full.

### Waist strategy
Define the waist with belts at the natural waistline, nipped seams, princess seams, wrap silhouettes, or darker color blocking through the midsection. The waist is the central balance point of the silhouette, so most outfits benefit from a deliberate marker there.

### Shoulder/hip balance
The silhouette is already balanced and does not need volume added at either pole. Avoid stacking volume on both top and bottom simultaneously, which obscures the waist.

### Vertical line
Optional. The natural curve already reads as balanced, so column dressing or open jackets are flexible tools rather than necessary corrections.

### Best rises
Mid- to high-rise bottoms sit at or just above the natural waist and reinforce the indent. Low-rise can drop the visual waist down onto the widest part of the hip.

### Best hemlines
Skirts and dresses at knee or longer; pants in straight, slightly tapered, or bootcut. Dramatic full skirts work because the defined waist anchors the volume.

### Necklines
V-neck, sweetheart, jewel, scoop, and gentle round necklines all balance well. Use carefully wide boat or Sabrina necklines, which add horizontal weight at the shoulder line and disrupt balance.

### Sleeves
Set-in fitted sleeves follow the natural curve. Cap, three-quarter, and gently puffed sleeves work. Use carefully drop-shoulder, dolman, and heavy structured shoulders, which widen the upper line.

### Layering rules
Single-breasted jackets that nip at the waist or hang open in a vertical line work consistently. Belted coats and wrap layering preserve the waist signal through outerwear.

### Fabric and structure
Lightweight, drapey, body-skimming knits and wovens trace the silhouette. Heavy or stiff fabric obscures the indentation. Fine-gauge knits read more cleanly than chunky knits.

### Use carefully
Boxy or shapeless silhouettes, drop-waist dresses, empire-line cuts that ignore the natural waist, and cropped jackets paired with bulky bottoms can flatten the curve.

### Common misidentifications
Confused with Rectangle when the bust is full but the waist is not actually indented to the 25% / 9 in threshold; resolve by measuring waist differential rather than relying on visual curve. Confused with Pear when low-hip carry is heavy; the X versus 8 distinction (below) often resolves this.

### Notes
Imogen Lamport distinguishes X from Figure 8 by where the widest point falls below the waist: X has its widest point at the thigh with a smooth slope; 8 has a "shelf" at the high hip immediately below the waist. Treat both as Hourglass for the canonical-five framework and surface the variant for skirt-shape recommendations.

### 1a. Top Hourglass

**ID:** `top-hourglass`
**Family:** variant of `hourglass`
**Aliases:** bust-dominant hourglass, hourglass top

**How to identify:** Hourglass overall, but bust/upper body slightly larger than hips. Bust exceeds hips by 1 to 10 in (FFIT). Waist still nipped at least 9 in below bust.
**Delta from Hourglass:** Direct visual emphasis toward the lower half to even the read.

### Diagnostic cues
- Bust pixel-width clearly larger than hip pixel-width, but waist still indents sharply.
- Distinguished from Inverted Triangle by waist definition: Top Hourglass keeps the ≥9 in waist-bust differential.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Favor V and scoop necklines that vertically break the chest.
- Lower-body volume helps: A-line skirts, slightly flared trousers, bootcut.
- Tailored jackets that flare from the waist add weight to the hip line.
- Use carefully: shoulder-volume sleeves, heavy embellishment on the shoulder line.

---

### 1b. Bottom Hourglass

**ID:** `bottom-hourglass`
**Family:** variant of `hourglass`
**Aliases:** hip-dominant hourglass, hourglass bottom

**How to identify:** Hourglass overall, but hips slightly wider than bust. Hips exceed bust by 3.6 to 10 in (FFIT), with waist still ≥9 in narrower than hips and high-hip-to-waist ratio under 1.193.
**Delta from Hourglass:** Direct visual emphasis upward to balance the lower half.

### Diagnostic cues
- Hip pixel-width clearly larger than bust, waist still nipped sharply.
- Distinguished from Pear by the persistence of a strongly defined waist (≥9 in differential), and a less dramatic hip-to-bust gap than a true Pear.
- Distinguished from Spoon by the absence of a high-hip shelf (high-hip/waist ratio under 1.193).

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Build interest at neckline and shoulder: structured shoulders, wider necklines, lighter color above the waist.
- Pencil and straight skirts work better than fit-and-flare, which can amplify the hip differential.
- Use carefully: hip pockets, heavy embellishment at the hip line, peplums that sit at the widest hip.

---

### 1c. Figure 8

**ID:** `figure-8`
**Family:** variant of `hourglass`
**Aliases:** 8-shape, shelf-hip hourglass, high-hip hourglass

**How to identify:** Defined waist with shoulders ≈ hips, but the widest point below the waist is the high hip just under the waistline rather than the thigh. The silhouette looks like a true 8: nip, bulge, nip, bulge.
**Delta from Hourglass:** Garments that flare from the high hip pull tight at the shelf; garments that fall straight from the high hip work.

### Diagnostic cues
- Widest pixel-width below waist falls within ~3 in of the waist itself.
- Often shorter through the body than X-shape Hourglass.
- Hip dips frequently visible at the high-hip transition.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Pencil, straight, and bias-cut skirts that fall from the high hip downward.
- Empire and waist details work; full circle and A-line skirts that flare from the high hip can strain across the shelf.
- Wide-leg trousers that fall straight from the high hip read well.

---

### 1d. Vase

**ID:** `vase`
**Family:** variant of `hourglass`
**Aliases:** tall hourglass, elongated hourglass

**How to identify:** Hourglass proportions on a notably tall, elongated frame with longer legs and longer torso.
**Delta from Hourglass:** Less need for elongation; risk shifts toward over-emphasizing the bust on a long frame.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Three-quarter sleeves and straight-leg jeans concentrate visual interest at slimmer points (forearm, ankle).
- Pencil skirts, fitted shirts, and polished tailoring read well.
- Use carefully: very high-cut sleeveless tops, fussy necklines, and turtlenecks, which can shorten the long line of the frame.

---

### 1e. Cello

**ID:** `cello`
**Family:** variant of `hourglass`
**Aliases:** plus-size hourglass, full hourglass

**How to identify:** Hourglass curves on a fuller frame, often with a short waist, large bust, full hips and thighs, and proportionally slim lower legs.
**Delta from Hourglass:** Lengthen the short waist and direct attention to slim lower legs.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Empire-waist halterneck dresses lengthen the short torso.
- Wide-leg trousers fall cleanly from the hip.
- Platform wedges, ankle-strap shoes, and shoes that don't bulk the foot keep the slim-calf advantage visible.
- Bold prints and structured details register well at scale.

## 2. Pear (Triangle / A-shape)

**ID:** `pear`
**Family:** primary
**Aliases:** Triangle, A-shape, Spoon (in some FFIT-derived systems)

**How to identify:** Hips visibly wider than shoulders/bust, with a defined waist that registers because the hips create the differential. The lower body carries more visual weight than the upper.
**Goal:** Build visual weight on the upper body to match the wider hips, while letting the natural waist read clearly.

### Diagnostic cues
- Hip pixel-width at least 5% wider than shoulder/bust pixel-width.
- FFIT thresholds: hips exceed bust by ≥3.6 in and <10 in, with hips ≥9 in wider than waist.
- Tummy generally flat; waist registers as defined even if the waist itself is not unusually small.
- Bust typically small to medium; shoulders can be sloped.
- Limbs often slimmer through the upper arm, fuller through the upper thigh.

### Waist strategy
Show the waist. This is one of the strongest features of the shape. Tuck, belt at the natural waist (not lower), wrap tops, and front-tucked tees all let the indent register.

### Shoulder/hip balance
Add visual weight above the waist with structured shoulders, lighter or brighter color, prints, embellishment, and bust-area detail. Keep the lower body cleaner with darker, plainer, fluid fabric.

### Vertical line
Long jackets and coats that hit at mid-thigh travel past the widest hip point and create a vertical that breaks up the hip line.

### Best rises
Mid- to high-rise. Low-rise sits at the widest point of the hip and amplifies it.

### Best hemlines
Skirts at the knee, between knee and mid-calf, or full-length. Pants in straight, ankle-length, bootcut, wide-leg, or flared cuts that fall straight from the widest hip.

### Necklines
Wide and lower: boat, square, bateau, off-shoulder, cowl. These broaden the upper line. Use carefully narrow V or U-necks if the bust is small and shoulders narrow, since they can read narrower than the hips.

### Sleeves
Short flutter, wide cap, bell, and cap sleeves add upper-body volume. Long sleeves with shoulder structure (puff, structured cap) and a taper toward the wrist work. Use carefully: long bell sleeves that mirror the hip flare.

### Layering rules
Cropped jackets ending above the widest hip, or long jackets ending well below it. Use carefully: jackets that end exactly at the widest hip line, which underscore the broadest point.

### Fabric and structure
Structured fabric on top (twill, ponte, denim with body), fluid fabric below (matte jersey, drapey crepe). Place pockets, prints, and embellishment above the waist; keep the lower half plain.

### Use carefully
Skinny tapered pants and jeans that cling to the widest part; whiskering and pocket detail at the hip; horizontal hem detail at the thigh; peplums that land at the widest hip.

### Common misidentifications
Confused with Hourglass when the bust is large enough to read balanced; resolve by checking whether hips are more than 5% wider than shoulders. Confused with Rectangle when waist definition is mild. Imogen Lamport's 8-shape can read as Pear when shoulders are narrow; resolve by checking whether the widest point below the waist is the high hip (8) or the thigh (Pear).

### Notes
Sloped shoulders read narrower than they measure, which can exaggerate the apparent Pear differential. A well-fitted bra changes the visual bust line and can shift the read.

### 2a. Spoon

**ID:** `spoon`
**Family:** variant of `pear`
**Aliases:** shelf-hip pear, FFIT spoon

**How to identify:** Pear with a distinct horizontal "shelf" or ledge at the high hip just below the waist, often with lower-belly fullness sitting forward. The high-hip-to-waist ratio is at least 1.193 in FFIT terms.
**Delta from Pear:** Garments that flare from the natural waist strain across the shelf; clean falls from above the shelf work better.

### Diagnostic cues
- Side profile shows a forward lower-belly curve below the waist.
- High hip is wider than low hip in many cases.
- A visible step or ledge transition from waist to hip from the front.
- Hip dips often visible at the side of the high hip.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Mid- to high-rise bottoms with wide waistbands that sit above the lower belly.
- Bias-cut midi skirts that glide past the shelf rather than flare from the waist.
- Empire-waist, wrap, and fit-and-flare dresses with the seam above the shelf.
- Soft peplums starting above the lower belly, not at the natural waist.
- Use carefully: low-rise jeans, whiskered jeans across the lower belly, A-line skirts that flare from a too-high point.

### Notes
Sources disagree on whether Spoon is a synonym for Pear or a distinct shelf-hip variant. The shelf-hip definition (FFIT, PowerSutra, Truekung) is more diagnostically useful and is adopted here.

---

### 2b. Skittle

**ID:** `skittle`
**Family:** variant of `pear`
**Aliases:** bowling-pin shape

**How to identify:** Slim, long upper body with average bust and small defined waist, paired with chunkier all-around thighs and calves and a flat smaller bottom. No saddlebag distribution.
**Delta from Pear:** Lower-body fullness is in the thighs themselves rather than the hip/saddle area, so balance shifts to thigh-skimming silhouettes.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Wide-leg pants balance the thighs without clinging.
- Angled pockets rather than side pockets keep the thigh line clean.
- The upper body is the asset; let it carry tailored, elegant tops.
- Use carefully: baggy whole-body cover-ups that obscure the slim torso.

---

### 2c. Bell

**ID:** `bell`
**Family:** variant of `pear`
**Aliases:** narrow-shoulder pear, short-waisted pear

**How to identify:** Small or sloped shoulders, small bust, short and small waist, large bottom and thighs, often with a shorter torso and longer legs. Upper body reads petite relative to lower body.
**Delta from Pear:** Less waist definition than a true Pear, so the strategy leans more on shoulder-broadening than waist-defining.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Cowl-neck tops, statement necklaces, and metallic or printed neckline detail draw the eye up.
- Subtle ruffles or puffs at the shoulder broaden the upper line.
- Funnel and A-line coats fall cleanly past the lower body.
- Use carefully: deep wide V-necks (read as too revealing on a small bust), foppish sleeves, and fussy lower-body detail.

## 3. Inverted Triangle (V-shape)

**ID:** `inverted-triangle`
**Family:** primary
**Aliases:** V-shape, Strawberry, Heart, Cornet (Trinny and Susannah), athletic, swimmer's build

**How to identify:** Shoulders and/or bust visibly wider than hips, with little waist definition or with a waist that is overshadowed by broad shoulders. The upper torso carries more visual weight than the lower.
**Goal:** Soften and narrow the upper body; add volume and visual interest to the hips and lower body.

### Diagnostic cues
- Shoulder/bust pixel-width at least 5% wider than hip pixel-width.
- FFIT thresholds: bust exceeds hips by ≥3.6 in, with bust-waist differential under 9 in.
- The widest horizontal point on the upper body is the shoulder/clavicle line; this distinguishes from Apple, where the widest point is the mid-torso.
- Hips often narrow with a flatter bottom and slimmer legs.
- Bust may range from small to large; broad shoulders alone are sufficient for the diagnosis.

### Waist strategy
If the waist is defined, mark it with a belt or a wrap. If not, bypass it with empire definition or fluid tops that float away from the midsection. Imogen Lamport explicitly endorses the "surrender the waistline" approach for this shape.

### Shoulder/hip balance
Subtract from the top: dark or quiet color, soft drapey fabric, clean uncluttered shoulders. Add to the bottom: brighter color, prints, pockets, structured volume.

### Vertical line
Narrow vertical lines on the upper body (open jackets, columns of tonal color) reduce shoulder visual width.

### Best rises
Mid-rise as a default. High-rise works if the waist is defined or to anchor a fuller skirt.

### Best hemlines
Skirts can run extreme: very short and flared or long and flowing. A-line, full, bias, tulip, tiered, and dropped-waist skirts add lower-body volume.

### Necklines
Slim and long: deep V, U-neck, scoop, asymmetric, halter (for smaller bust). The vertical break softens the shoulder line. Use carefully: boat, bateau, off-shoulder, square, Sabrina, and sweetheart necklines, which broaden the shoulder.

### Sleeves
Short fitted or tapered sleeves let shoulders read narrower. Long raglan, kimono, and dolman sleeves shift visual weight downward. Use carefully: puff sleeves, structured cap sleeves, epaulettes, and shoulder pads.

### Layering rules
Single-breasted, longer jackets with detail at hip level (pockets, flare, hem volume). Use carefully: double-breasted jackets, big collars, structured-shoulder coats.

### Fabric and structure
Soft and weighted on top (drapey jersey, fine knit); fuller and structured on bottom (heavier weave, pleating, gathered fullness). Bold prints belong below the waist.

### Use carefully
Skinny pants that under-balance the hips; horizontal stripes on top; shoulder embellishment; thick set-in shoulders.

### Common misidentifications
Confused with Apple (both top-heavy from the front): V has narrow hips and a flatter midsection, Apple has fuller mid-torso and hips closer to shoulder width. Confused with Rectangle when the shoulder differential is mild. Confused with Hourglass when the V also has a defined waist; resolve by hip-to-shoulder differential.

### Notes
Some V-shapes have a defined waist and benefit from blended advice that combines V upper-body strategy with X waist treatment.

### 3a. Lollipop

**ID:** `lollipop`
**Family:** variant of `inverted-triangle`
**Aliases:** top-heavy column, bust-prominent inverted triangle

**How to identify:** Large but not heavy bust, slim hips, proportionally small waist, long elegant legs. The upper-body curve is concentrated at the bust rather than the shoulder bone, and the lower body is notably slender.
**Delta from Inverted Triangle:** The dominant feature is bust prominence rather than shoulder breadth, so styling adds lower-body width primarily and softens bust profile rather than shoulder width.

### Diagnostic cues
- Defined waist visible.
- Slim legs visible in a full-body shot.
- Distinguished from Cornet: Cornet has a small bust and shoulder-driven breadth; Lollipop has bust-driven breadth.
- Distinguished from Hourglass and Vase: hips are notably narrower than the bust, not balanced.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Skirts with hip detail, fluted skirts, flared trousers add lower-body weight.
- Full-length coats that button under the bust create a vertical from bust to hem.
- Slim heels lengthen the leg line.
- Use carefully: polo necks, chunky knitwear, and anything that hangs loose from the bust line.

---

### 3b. Cornet (Strawberry)

**ID:** `cornet`
**Family:** variant of `inverted-triangle` (canonical example)
**Aliases:** Strawberry, athletic, V-shape, Heart, Carrot

**How to identify:** Broad squared shoulders, small breasts, little waist definition, slim narrow hips, long slim legs. This is the canonical Inverted Triangle as represented in most modern stylist literature.
**Delta from Inverted Triangle:** None significant; this variant is the textbook case.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Fitted upper bodies suggest a waistline where bone structure does not.
- Heels draw the eye to the slim leg line.
- Use carefully: shapeless tops that hang from the shoulder, shoulder pads, and shoulder embellishment.

---

### 3c. Goblet

**ID:** `goblet`
**Family:** variant of `inverted-triangle`
**Aliases:** none widely standardized

**How to identify:** Broad shoulders, wide back, large bust, some midsection fullness, no defined waist, slim long legs, smaller bottom.
**Delta from Inverted Triangle:** Adds bust and mid-torso fullness on top of the shoulder breadth, so the strategy blends Inverted Triangle and Apple advice.

### Diagnostic cues
- Widest point is the shoulder line, but mid-torso also reads full.
- Distinguished from Apple: Apple's widest point is mid-torso, Goblet's is shoulders.
- Distinguished from Cornet: Goblet has large bust and some midsection fullness; Cornet has small bust and slim torso.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Show the legs (the asset): shorter skirts, heels, narrow ankle bottoms.
- Supportive bras lift the bust line away from the waist and create vertical separation.
- Use carefully: boxy or square upper-body styling that emphasizes both shoulder and torso width.

## 4. Rectangle (Banana / Straight / H-shape)

**ID:** `rectangle`
**Family:** primary
**Aliases:** Banana, Straight, Ruler, H-shape, Athletic build

**How to identify:** Bust, waist, and hips read as roughly the same width, with little inward indentation at the waist. The torso silhouette traces a column from shoulder to hip.
**Goal:** Either break the column with deliberate curve cues at bust and hip, or accept the column and lengthen with vertical lines.

### Diagnostic cues
- Bust, waist, and hip pixel-widths all within 5% of one another.
- Waist less than 25% smaller than bust/hips, or under 9 in differential (FFIT).
- The most common shape in the population (≈46% in NC State SizeUSA data).
- Bust prominence varies; busty Rectangles are commonly misclassified as Hourglass (the visual curve from bust mass can mislead).
- Limbs typically read in proportion with the torso column.

### Waist strategy
Sources disagree here, and the right approach depends on torso length and frame width:
- For longer, leaner Rectangles (Imogen Lamport's I-shape): belt and define the waist freely; wrap dresses and nipped jackets work.
- For wider or shorter Rectangles (Imogen Lamport's H-shape): bypass the waist; longer tops, drop-waist details, and unbroken vertical lines avoid emphasizing the boxy silhouette.
- For shapes between the two: use a soft waist marker (sash, tonal belt) rather than a hard contrast belt.

### Shoulder/hip balance
Already balanced. Either add curves at both bust and hip with prints, details, peplum, and volume; or keep the column intact and lengthen with vertical lines and column dressing.

### Vertical line
Long open coats and cardigans, long necklaces, columns of tonal color, vertical seaming, and center-front detailing all create the elongating effect this shape benefits most from.

### Best rises
Low- to mid-rise are commonly recommended because high-rise can emphasize the undefined waist. This contradicts the high-rise default for most other shapes, so any app using a single rise default should override here.

### Best hemlines
Tops that end below the natural waist (long enough to bypass the waist line). Long jackets past the knee for elongation. Skirts in any length but with detail or flare to break the column.

### Necklines
Both wider necklines (V, sweetheart, slash, scoop) for curve creation, and narrow necklines (turtleneck, mandarin, halter, funnel) for a slender column effect. Use carefully: square and Sabrina necklines that add squareness on a wider frame.

### Sleeves
Embellished, loose, wide: princess, puff, flutter, flared, cuffed. These introduce visual interest and break the column. Use carefully: plain fitted set-in sleeves that mirror the column line.

### Layering rules
Long jackets longer than hip-length, long open cardigans (sometimes belted), drop-waist seams. Double-breasted styles can work because they break up the front line. Use carefully (for H-shape specifically): peplums and waist-seamed wrap dresses.

### Fabric and structure
Bold prints, contrasting textures, varied stripes, and ruching all introduce visual breakup. Mix structured outer pieces with fluid inner pieces to suggest dimension where the body line is straight.

### Use carefully
Tight-fitting columns that emphasize the column; boxy oversized shapes that read shapeless; clinging fabrics with no shaping detail.

### Common misidentifications
Confused with Hourglass when the bust is full but the waist is not actually indented to threshold; resolve by measuring waist-bust differential. Confused with Pear when hips are mildly larger; resolve via the 5% threshold. Confused with Inverted Triangle when shoulders read athletic; resolve by hip-shoulder differential.

### Notes
The H/I sub-distinction matters for waist treatment recommendations. The common stylist advice "wrap dresses suit everyone" is contested by Imogen Lamport, who notes wrap dresses can emphasize an undefined waist on H-shape Rectangles.

### 4a. Column

**ID:** `column`
**Family:** variant of `rectangle`
**Aliases:** I-shape, Ruler, slim straight

**How to identify:** Tall and slim Rectangle with shoulders ≈ hips, little waist definition, flat tummy and bottom, and long legs. Often petite or fine-boned.
**Delta from Rectangle:** Belting and waist details work because the silhouette is slim enough not to read boxy when cinched.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Belts at the natural waist work and are not contraindicated as in H-shape.
- Dramatic angular cuts on the upper body suggest curve.
- Round-neck pullovers, cropped trousers, A-line skirts, and flared trousers all introduce shape.
- Use carefully: shapeless straight-down clothing that reads as sack rather than column.

---

### 4b. Brick

**ID:** `brick`
**Family:** variant of `rectangle`
**Aliases:** square shape

**How to identify:** Broad shoulders ≈ hips, no defined waist, small or no bottom, thick thighs and calves, small bust. The frame reads stockier and squarer than Column or standard Rectangle.
**Delta from Rectangle:** The silhouette already reads square, so styling avoids cuts that emphasize squareness.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Tightly belted jackets at the natural waist suggest a waist where the bone structure does not.
- Empire-line coats and gently draping jersey tops soften the square upper line.
- Asymmetrical skirts and patterned tops break up the squareness with diagonal or busy lines.
- Use carefully: bulky broad-shouldered jackets, boxy square cuts, and rigid horizontal lines.

## 5. Apple (Round / O-shape)

**ID:** `apple`
**Family:** primary
**Aliases:** Round, O-shape, Oval (when used interchangeably)

**How to identify:** The midsection reads as the widest visual point of the torso, with little to no waist indentation. Bust and hips often read roughly equal to one another, but the waist is wider than both. Side profile typically shows tummy projection forward of the bust line.
**Goal:** Skim the midsection and redirect the eye upward to the bust, neckline, and face, and downward to the legs.

### Diagnostic cues
- Waist measurement equal to or greater than bust and hip measurements (Sokolowski plus-size FFIT modification).
- Bust and hips often within ~5% of one another.
- In side profile, abdominal projection is forward of the bust line; this is the strongest single diagnostic.
- Limbs (arms and legs) often read proportionally slimmer than the torso.
- Bust prominence varies and is not required for the diagnosis.

### Waist strategy
Bypass the natural waist. Most sources agree that belting at the natural waist amplifies the widest point; some sources allow a light empire belt under the bust. The empire seam should be smooth rather than gathered (gathered empire reads as maternity).

### Shoulder/hip balance
Hips and shoulders are roughly equal. Use the décolletage and the legs as the focal anchors rather than building hip volume.

### Vertical line
Critical for this shape. Long open jackets, dusters, and cardigans, columns of tonal color, long necklaces, and vertical seam detail all draw the eye up and down past the midsection.

### Best rises
High-rise sits above the widest point and provides smooth coverage. Mid-rise is acceptable. Low-rise cuts directly at the widest part of the midsection.

### Best hemlines
Knee-length or longer dresses to show the leg as anchor. Pants in straight, wide, or bootcut cuts. Showing the calf and ankle keeps the leg as a visible focal point.

### Necklines
V-neck has the strongest source consensus: it creates the strongest vertical break at the chest and elongates the torso. Scoop, sweetheart, wrap, and asymmetric necklines also work. Use carefully: turtleneck, mock-neck, funnel, and crew necklines, which truncate the vertical and emphasize midsection breadth.

### Sleeves
Three-quarter sleeves cut the line at a balancing point and draw the eye to the wrist and forearm. Set-in or slightly bell sleeves work; structured shoulder gives an upper-body anchor. Use carefully: very voluminous sleeves on already-broader shoulders.

### Layering rules
Open jackets, dusters, and longline cardigans that extend past the midsection are central. Single-breasted only: double-breasted adds horizontal bulk across the midsection. Length should be longer than hip-length to skim past the midsection. Use carefully: cropped jackets that end at the waist.

### Fabric and structure
Soft, fluid, draping fabrics that skim rather than cling. Matte jersey, ponte, twill, crepe, and chiffon. Use carefully: stretch knits that cling, stiff fabrics that hold a horizontal line, and very thin clingy jersey.

### Use carefully
Gathered empire styles directly under the bust; cropped tops; tucked-in shirts without an open layer; horizontal stripes at the waist line; thin spaghetti straps that read out of scale.

### Common misidentifications
Confused with Inverted Triangle (both top-heavy from the front): Apple's widest point is the midsection and hips ≈ shoulders; Inverted Triangle's widest point is the shoulder line and hips are narrower than shoulders. Confused with Rectangle (both lack waist definition): Apple has waist > bust/hips; Rectangle has waist ≈ bust ≈ hips. A side-profile photo is the most reliable single tool for resolving these confusions.

### Notes
Apples often emerge from former Hourglass or Rectangle shapes after weight gain or post-menopausal redistribution. Limbs often remain slim while torso fills, so checking arm and leg proportion against torso is a strong diagnostic.

### 5a. Diamond

**ID:** `diamond`
**Family:** variant of `apple`
**Aliases:** Kite, mid-fullness

**How to identify:** Narrow shoulders and narrow hips with a full midsection that sits high and tight. The waist is the widest point. Slim shapely legs are common.
**Delta from Apple:** Add width at both shoulder and hip lines (the opposite of the Apple shoulder-neutral default), since the shoulders and hips are narrower than the waist rather than balanced with it.

### Diagnostic cues
- Widest point is the waist/midsection.
- Shoulders narrower than waist; hips narrower than waist (this is the key distinction from Apple).
- Stomach sits high and tight rather than low and rounded.
- Slim shapely legs visible.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Add structured shoulders, boat necks, and statement collars to widen the upper line.
- Widen the hip line with full skirts or wider-leg pants.
- Empire-waist tops and dresses with smooth seams; V-necks for vertical break.
- Use carefully: belts at the waist (the widest point).

### Notes
Source definitions vary. The dominant definition (Roberta Lee, Who's On The Move, Fashioned Travel) uses narrow-shoulders + waist-as-widest + slim hips/legs. A minority definition (Sumissura, Healthline) describes Diamond as wider hips + full midsection, which conflates Diamond with Pear-with-belly. The dominant definition is used here.

---

### 5b. Oval

**ID:** `oval`
**Family:** variant of `apple`
**Aliases:** Round, full all-over

**How to identify:** Full all over with rounded torso, weight distributed evenly around midsection rather than projecting forward. Less defined waist than Apple. Stomach sits low and rounded (rather than high and tight as in Diamond).
**Delta from Apple:** Same general principles, but the low-set stomach changes which bottoms work.

### Diagnostic cues
- Distinguished from Diamond: Oval has low rounded stomach; Diamond has high tight stomach.
- Distinguished from Apple: Oval is fuller all over rather than front-loaded specifically.
- Often a longer torso than typical Apple.

### Styling adjustments from parent
- Use carefully: banded or elasticated bottoms that emphasize the low-set stomach.
- Loose draped tops with straight or wider shoulders work.
- Jackets that fall no shorter than the hip bone preserve the vertical line past the lower belly.

### Notes
Many sources use Oval and Apple interchangeably. The distinction surfaces only when separating from Diamond or designing low-stomach-specific styling rules.

## Universal silhouette principles

### Proportion math (rule of thirds, golden ratio)

Outfits divided into thirds (1:2 splits) read as more dynamic than 50/50 splits, which the eye perceives as static. The visual break can fall at the natural waist, the high hip, or the lower-thigh fingertip line. The golden ratio (1:1.618) is the mathematical parent of the rule of thirds and gives slightly more elongated proportions; in practice the rule of thirds is the more usable approximation.

The strongest break points are just below the bust (empire), the natural waist, the high hip, and just above or below the knee. **Mid-calf is the most challenging hem** because it tends to fall at the leg's widest visible point. The fashion croquis used in illustration is conventionally 9 heads tall (with the extra height added almost entirely below the waist), while real adult bodies are typically 7 to 8 heads tall. The 9-head ideal is an artistic convention, not a target proportion.

### Vertical line creation

Vertical lines guide the eye up and down quickly, so the figure reads as longer. The most reliable tools are center-front detailing (button plackets, princess seams, vertical pleats), long open layers (unbuttoned longline cardigans, dusters, blazers), V and scoop necklines, monochrome column dressing, narrow closely-spaced vertical stripes, and long narrow accessories (long necklaces, narrow scarves worn long).

Note: Helmholtz illusion research (Thompson and Mikellidou, 2011) showed in lab conditions that horizontal stripes can appear narrower than vertical stripes. The popular "verticals lengthen" rule remains practically reliable on a curved 3D body because of placement and accumulated cultural perception, but app guidance should treat it as a rule of thumb rather than a universal optical truth.

### Horizontal line management

A horizontal line in clothing stops the eye and draws attention to the spot where it falls. The eye also fills in horizontals between markers (sleeve hems aligned with pocket placements register as a virtual horizontal even with no literal stripe). Place horizontals where the eye should land (a tonal belt at the natural waist defines the waist; a contrast collar lifts the eye to the face). Avoid stacking horizontals at the same body level when that level is not the intended focal area. Width and contrast matter more than direction: wide high-contrast stripes widen wherever they sit regardless of orientation.

### Layering hierarchy

Five principles unify layering practice:

1. **Length progression**: each visible layer ends at a distinctly different point on the body. Identical hems read as a flat block.
2. **Weight progression**: thin or lightweight closest to the body, weight increases outward.
3. **Rule of three**: most layered outfits resolve at three visible layers (base, mid, accent).
4. **Color value progression**: outer layers are commonly darker than inner, framing the silhouette, but this is the most flexible rule.
5. **Sleeve layering**: visible hinge points (a cuff peeking from a sweater sleeve) signal intentionality; identical sleeve hems create a virtual horizontal at the wrist.

### Fit hierarchy (fitted plus flowing combinations)

The most consistent principle across image consulting and stylist literature is the one-fitted-plus-one-fluid rule: volume on top pairs with structure on bottom, and volume on bottom pairs with fitted on top. All-fitted can read as costumey or aggressively body-conscious; all-volume can obscure the underlying figure. Both extremes are valid editorial choices but require deliberate counter-cues (a defined waist, a strong shoe, a sharp neckline) to remain legible. The constant safety net is defining at least one anchor point: a belt, a tucked hem, an exposed wrist or ankle, an open neckline.

### Color and pattern as silhouette tools

Lighter colors visually advance and expand an area; darker colors recede. Lighter on top with darker on bottom shifts focus upward toward the face; the reverse shifts focus downward. Horizontal color blocks create a strong horizontal at the meeting line, which works when that line falls at an intended break (such as the natural waist) and works less well when it falls at the widest point. Vertical color blocking (contrasting side panels, an open jacket revealing a contrasting under-layer) creates verticals and is used to balance shapes asymmetrically. Monochromatic column dressing is the single most reliable elongating tool: one hue or one tonal family head-to-toe removes horizontal breaks. Texture variation within a column adds dimension without breaking the vertical.

### Print scale by shape

Print size should relate to the visual scale of the wearer's frame. Smaller frames typically register small to medium prints in scale; oversized prints can dominate the frame. Larger or taller frames register medium to bold prints at full strength; very small prints can disappear into the body. Mid-range frames have the widest range of working scales. Placement matters as much as scale: print concentrated at one body zone draws the eye there. Plus-size body-positive sources reject the legacy advice that fuller figures should only wear small dark prints; medium and large prints can be balanced and expressive when scale matches.

### Source disagreements and how they were resolved

1. **Spoon definition**: Some sources (Healthline, FFIT-derived) treat Spoon as synonymous with Pear; others (PowerSutra, Truekung, the FFIT high-hip ratio rule itself) treat Spoon as a distinct shelf-hip variant with high-hip-to-waist ratio ≥1.193. **Resolution: adopt the shelf-hip definition**, which is more diagnostically useful and matches the FFIT formula.
2. **Diamond definition**: Dominant definition (Roberta Lee, Who's On The Move) is narrow shoulders + waist as widest + slim hips and legs. Minority definition (Sumissura, Healthline) describes Diamond as wider hips with full midsection, conflating with Pear. **Resolution: adopt the dominant definition.**
3. **Oval vs Apple**: Many sources treat as identical. Some distinguish (Roberta Lee, Who's On The Move): Apple = high forward stomach; Oval = full all over with low rounded stomach. **Resolution: treat Oval as a sub-variant of Apple, surfaced when distinguishing from Diamond or designing low-belly-specific styling.**
4. **Rectangle waist treatment**: The Concept Wardrobe and Trinny and Susannah recommend belting and defining the waist; Imogen Lamport's H-shape advice rejects belting at the waist for wider Rectangles. **Resolution: treat as conditional on torso length and frame width** (Column/I = belt freely; Brick/H = bypass).
5. **Apple neckline**: V-neck has strong consensus, but scoop is widely recommended too. **Resolution: frame as "low/wide neckline" cluster rather than V-only.**
6. **Apple belting**: Some sources allow light empire belting; others (Imogen Lamport, Glance) caution against. **Resolution: under-bust empire (smooth, not gathered) is endorsed; natural-waist belting is "use carefully."**
7. **Pear necklines**: Concept Wardrobe favors wide and low (boat, square, off-shoulder); some sources recommend V-necks for upper-body elongation. **Resolution: not contradictory — wide adds upper-body width, V lengthens; surface both based on bust size and shoulder slope.**
8. **Bust vs shoulder as defining axis**: Imogen Lamport explicitly argues body shape should be defined by shoulders, waist, and hips (bone structure) rather than bust, waist, and hips (which include soft tissue). Most other systems use bust. **Resolution for a photo-based app**: shoulders are more reliable than bust because shoulders depend on skeletal structure rather than bra and posture.

### Confidence levels

- **High confidence** (multiple peer-reviewed or consensus sources, consistent across systems): Hourglass, Pear, Inverted Triangle, Rectangle, Apple core definitions; FFIT inch thresholds; the 5%/25% percentage system; rule of thirds; one-fitted-plus-one-fluid principle.
- **High confidence within named system**: Trinny and Susannah's 12 shapes (Apple, Brick, Goblet, Hourglass, Vase, Cello, Pear, Skittle, Bell, Column, Lollipop, Cornet); Imogen Lamport's 7 letters (X, 8, A, V, H, I, O).
- **Medium confidence** (definitions vary across sources): Spoon, Diamond, Oval as distinct from Apple, Bell as distinct from Pear and Spoon.
- **Lower confidence** (popular but undocumented thresholds): retailer fit-finder shape outputs (Calvin Klein, Stitch Fix); Healthline/WebMD apple-pear (useful for health framing but not styling-grade).

### Limitations

**Photo-specific limitations** (critical for app design):

1. **Circumference vs width**: All FFIT thresholds use circumferences (full ellipse perimeters). A photo gives 2D widths only (one axis of the ellipse). Two people with the same hip-front-width can have very different hip circumferences depending on glute projection. App must approximate circumference from silhouette and pose-and-shape priors (SMPL/SMPL-X-style).
2. **Front view alone misses depth**. Apple, Oval, and Diamond distinctions require side profile because abdominal projection is the strongest single diagnostic and is invisible from the front.
3. **Bone structure vs soft tissue**. A wide upper body in a photo may be wide shoulders (skeletal, → Inverted Triangle) or full bust and back (soft tissue, → can occur in any shape). Photo cannot reliably distinguish, but stylist recommendations differ. Recommend resolving with secondary cues (waist definition, hip width, side profile).
4. **Pose effects**. Stance width inflates hip pixel-width 3 to 8%; arm position changes apparent shoulder width 5 to 15%; spine curvature distorts apparent waist; weight shift to one leg breaks symmetry. Capture protocol must specify feet together, arms slightly out, neutral standing pose.
5. **Camera and lens effects**. Camera distance under ~2m introduces lens distortion; below-chest camera height enlarges hips, above-chest enlarges shoulders; wide-angle phone lenses bow vertical lines.
6. **Clothing**. Loose tops conceal waist definition entirely → systematic bias toward Rectangle. Bras, belts, and waistbands all alter the visible silhouette. Best practice: fitted activewear or swimsuit against neutral background.
7. **40% reclassification rate** (Parker et al. 2021): even with 3D body scans, slight differences in measurement landmark placement reclassify up to 40% of women into a different FFIT shape. A 2D-photo classifier will face wider uncertainty. **App should output probabilities with primary plus secondary shape, not single hard labels.**
8. **Population calibration**: FFIT distribution from NC State SizeUSA (n=6,318) is roughly 46% Rectangle, 20% Pear/Spoon, 14% Inverted Triangle, 8% Hourglass. A model predicting Hourglass at >15% is likely over-detecting waist definition. Distribution does not generalize cross-culturally.

**Recommended app capture protocol**: chest-height camera, ~2m distance, neutral background, fitted activewear or swimsuit, arms slightly away from body, feet together, neutral standing pose, even lighting, both front and side photos, optional height entry to anchor shape estimation. Output primary shape with confidence interval and secondary candidate.

**Tone and framing limitations**: Most legacy stylist sources use "flatter / hide / disguise" language. The principles can be extracted and reframed in balance terms (where does the eye travel, what creates rhythm, where is the focal anchor) without loss of technical content. Mathis and Connor, AICI training materials, and Imogen Lamport explicitly position the work as harmony and self-acceptance rather than correction; their framing was prioritized in this document.
